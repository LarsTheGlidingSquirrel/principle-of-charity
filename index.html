On the

Principle of Charity

or interpreting others in the most reasonable and charitable way

There are many possible interpretations Not only based on the words but also on tone, body language, setting, ... Also, your past experience, personality and what you know about the other affect your interpretations.
Hold back ... and don't react based on the first thing that comes to your mind. Even if it feels like it must be true. If we first allow other possibilities, we might realize we are not quite so sure any more about or initial interpretations.
Be open to being wrong Make some room in your head for the possibility that what you think is wrong. My first thought is that Charly wants us to stand guard in winter. But, maybe I am wrong and he has a different plan.
Explore possible interpretations Try to find new interpretations and look into those that you already have.
Ask questionsFor example, ask them to give more details. Or, start with an interpretation you think most likely and ask them about it.
Think Take some time to think about how to interpret something and listen to your subconscious thoughts and feelings.
 During a conversation it might not be appropriate to pause and think. But, you can ask questions and think meanwhile or you can think out loudly. Also, in most cases you can come back to a conversation later after you had some time to think.
Get help from others Seek out the help of others because they might have an outside perspective or know something you don't.
Acknowledge your bias Watch out! While interpreting, we tend be biased and think in ways we are already familiar.
For example, we might stick to a certain view because it is the first thing that came to our mind.
Bias overview Have an open mind Try to also find interpretations that do not come naturally to you. Even if they seem implausible first, don't reject them immediately and try to find a version that seems reasonable. Listen When somebody is talking try to listen carefully and put your focus on understanding what they say instead of on coming up with an answer.
Be charitable when you interpret others ...
Assume good intentions Assume the other is not acting malicious and with good intentions. Especially, if you do not know them well enough personally. Learn from one another Assume there is something you (and they) can learn. Find common ground Try to figure out what both of you have in common first instead of focusing on the differences. Don't think in boxes We can be quick to put others in boxes or associate them with groups. Instead, focus on the individual.
Forgive mistakes Don't get caught up with unimportant details even if you spot a mistake or a logical fallacy. Instead, focus your attention on what is important.
First understand, then react Now that you understand what the other wants to say you can formulate an answer.
Be constructive Think about what you could say to make sure both of you leave the conversation better off than before. Disagree If you disagree you can now find a good counter argument that addresses their point
The Principle of Charity is still helpful if you end up disagreeing. Learn Maybe you learned something or discovered an argument that influenced your view. Step away Maybe you just want to not engage further now that you understood it.
